# About Me

It started with a question: how does this thing actually work? Then Slowly i started to realize that i could make computers do any work that i want and just as in Minecraft we say "I Realized I Could Become a God of My Machine" Not just on the surface, but at the seams — the protocols, the handshakes, the tiny decisions that shape whether a system bends or breaks.

Code and Terminal became a medium of Communication. Vulnerable machines in a home lab became puzzles. Bug bounty reports became documentation of curiosity. Now, every layer of a network feels like a paragraph waiting to be read.

But I'm not just a technical person — I'm a storyteller. I write about security the way a journalist would: accessible, contextual, human. The most dangerous vulnerabilities are the ones no one understands well enough to talk about.

Music keeps me grounded. Literature keeps me honest. Research keeps me sharp. And at 2am, with headphones in and a terminal open, all of it comes together.

# Research & Curiosity

Web Application Security
The web is the most attacked surface in the world — and still one of the least understood by the developers building it. I'm drawn to the gap between what frameworks promise and what they actually protect.
▶ OWASP Top 10 deep-dives, PortSwigger Web Security Academy
Digital Forensics
Every compromised system tells a story. Learning to reconstruct it — from log artifacts, memory dumps, and disk images — feels like investigative journalism for machines.
▶ Volatility framework, disk imaging tools, CTF forensics
Privacy & Surveillance Studies
Security is political. The tools we build and regulate determine who gets watched, by whom, and why. This intersection of tech and policy keeps me reading.
▶ EFF reports, metadata leakage in communications apps
Malware Analysis
Understanding how malware thinks — persistence mechanisms, obfuscation, C2 channels — is the precondition to understanding how to defend against it.
▶ Any.run sandbox analysis, threat intel write-ups
Cyber Psychology
Most breaches happen because of people, not code. Studying why humans make predictable decisions under uncertainty feels as important as any technical vulnerability.
▶ Behavioral security research, social engineering case studies
